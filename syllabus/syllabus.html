    Course Content and Structure.

    According to the course catalog, this course covers “Linear systems, matrices, subspaces of Euclidean spaces, linear transformations on Euclidean spaces, eigenvalues, eigenvectors”. These topics may sound abstract (and can certainly be presented that way), but also have deep and meaningful applications to the world around us. Our goal will be to understand these topics as mathematical objects, but also give proper attention to their usefulness around us.
    🔗
    The mathematician Carl Cowen said that “no significant use of linear algebra happens without a computer”, so we’ll also give attention to developing our ability to deploy linear algebraic thinking to solve realistic problems using a computer. Our daily course meetings will emphasize active learning and a collaborative work environment. Please come to each meeting prepared with any preparatory work, a device to access our course textbook and activities, and a willingness to actively participate.

    Section Assignments, Assessments, and Grades

    Learning Objectives.

    Our assignments and assessments will emphasize the following learning objectives:
    1. I visited an office hour in the first 4 weeks of class.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. I can execute the next 2-3 steps of Gaussian elimination by hand that are needed to work toward RREF (and recognize that additional steps are needed to achieve RREF), and identify which row operations were used. (Section 1.2)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. I can use information about pivots to make conclusions about the solutions of a system of linear equations, and explain the underlying reasoning (using appropriate terminology) regarding the existence and uniqueness of solutions and consistency or inconsistency of a system. (Section 1.4)
      🔗
      🔗
    4. I can determine if and how a given vector can be written as a linear combination of a given set of vectors and explain what this means geometrically. (Section 2.1)
      🔗
      🔗
    5. I can represent a linear system in three different ways and be able to translate back and forth among the forms: as Ax = b, as a question about linear combinations, and as a question about an augmented matrix (Section 2.2 - Prop 2.2.4)
      🔗
      🔗
    6. I can reformulate and answer questions about span in terms of linear combinations, matrix equations, and geometrically. (Section 2.3)
      🔗
      🔗
    7. I can reformulate and answer questions about linearly dependent or linearly independent vectors using appropriate computations, and explain the meaning of the results of computations in the language of linear combinations. (Section 2.4)
      🔗
      🔗
    8. I can find the matrix of a linear transformation using elementary vectors, apply a transformation to a vector or shape, and compose transformations using matrix multiplication. (Section 2.5)
      🔗
      🔗
    9. I can determine if a matrix is invertible, find an inverse, and use inverses to solve matrix equations. (Section 3.1)
      🔗
      🔗
    10. I can determine whether or not a set of vectors is a basis for \(\mathbb{R}^n\text{,}\) and give examples and non-examples of bases. (Section 3.2)
      🔗
      🔗
    11. I can convert a vector given in the standard basis into a vector in another basis and vice versa. (Section 3.2)
      🔗
      🔗
    12. I can find the determinant of a given matrix using cofactor expansion and explain what information this tells us about the invertibility of a matrix. (Section 3.4)
      🔗
      🔗
    13. I can find and clearly justify bases for the null space and the column space of a matrix (including why the set of vectors is a basis, i.e., why it is linearly independent and spans the relevant spaces) and thus their dimensions. (Section 3.5)
      🔗
      🔗
    14. I can verify that a vector is an eigenvector algebraically and explain what this means about the vector geometrically. (Section 4.1)
      🔗
      🔗
    15. I can use the characteristic polynomial of a matrix A to identify its eigenvalues, and then find a basis of eigenvectors for each associated eigenspace and, if there is a basis of eigenvectors for \(\mathbb{R}^n\text{,}\) use such a basis to describe the matrix product \(A\mathbf{x}\text{.}\) (Section 4.2)

    Labs.

    About 6 times throughout the semester, you’ll start a “lab” in class that you’ll complete and write up outside of class for a formal grade. Labs are more involved activities that help explore certain topics more deeply. Normally these will be done in groups of 2 or 3 and assigned a single group grade.

    🔗

    Quizzes.

    We will have weekly proficiency quizzes addressing the list of learning objectives that measure your understanding of certain essential skills you need to build a deep understanding of linear algebra.
    🔗
    Most Fridays, we will have an in-class quiz that assesses a subset of these skills, which will be announced in advance. Each skill will be graded as either “P” for “Proficient” or “NY” for “not yet”. To earn a “P”, your solution needs to show all work and explanations, use correct notation, and arrive at a correct answer. In short, your work should be close to perfect. Once you have demonstrated proficiency on a learning target, you do not need to do so again. If you don’t demonstrate proficiency on a particular target on a particular quiz, you can try again the next week. I won’t provide a lot of detailed feedback because it’s very helpful for you to learn to assess your work independently of me. I am happy to talk with you about any of your work on the quizzes. Each target will appear on at least three quizzes. Since you have multiple attempts on each target, there in general will be no makeup quizzes. If you still haven’t demonstrated proficiency after your third attempt, you are welcome to talk with me about it. Your grade for this component will be the percentage of the learning targets for which you have demonstrated proficiency.
    🔗
    This is a very important component of our course for the following reasons:
    • Learning is a process. You will not be penalized if you don’t demonstrate proficiency in a skill the first time, but you are expected to do so eventually.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Frequent assessment is good for learning. Think of these quizzes as a tool for learning rather than just assessment.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Keeping up with course material is important. These quizzes will be one way of informing both you and me about your progress in the course.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Knowing the basics well is crucial for tackling more in-depth problems. These targets were chosen because they are fundamental so you should think of them as building blocks. You will have time to practice these skills before, during, and after class, but we will also spend class time using them to tackle substantial problems.

    Academic Integrity and the Honor Code.

    We learn best together, which is why there is a large amount of collaboration built into our course structure. However, there is a difference between learning together and using someone else’s work. If you are wondering if you crossed the line, ask yourself "Could I start over and redo this on my own, and would it basically look like this?" If not, then you are submitting someone else’s work (plagiarism). Copying solutions or statements from the internet wihthout citing your source also constitutes plagiarism. We take academic integrity seriously, so all cases of plagiarized work or cheating will receive a 0 and will be submitted to the SRC.
    🔗
    This course will adhere to the CSU Academic Integrity Policy as found in the Colorado State University General Catalog and the Student Conduct Code. At a minimum, violations will result in a grading penalty in this course and a report to the Office of Conflict Resolution and Student Conduct Services. See more details at the catalog and TILT.
