    Worksheet \(\S 2.5\text{:}\) Matrix Transformations and Composition
    

    1. Motivation: Composition.

    🔗
    Solution.
    Last time we were introduced to matrices as transformations (functions on vectors). Like functions in one variable, it is often useful to "chain" together transformations (composition).
    🔗
    Given two transformations \(T: \R^n \to \R^m\text{,}\) \(T(\xvec) = A\xvec\text{,}\) and \(S: \R^m \to \R^p\text{,}\) \(S(\xvec) = B\xvec\text{,}\) we can form the composition transformation
    \begin{equation*} (S \circ T) : \R^n \to \R^p, \,\, (S \circ T)(\xvec) = (BA)\xvec\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    2. Activity: Composition.

    We will explore the composition of matrix transformations by revisiting the matrix transformations from a previous activity.
    1. Let’s begin with the matrix transformation \(T:\real^2\to\real^2\) that rotates a two-dimensional vector \(\xvec\) by \(90^\circ\) to produce \(T(\xvec)\text{.}\) We saw in the earlier activity that the associated matrix is \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \amp -1 \\ 1 \amp 0 \\ \end{bmatrix} \text{.}\) Suppose that we compose this matrix transformation with itself to obtain \((T\circ T)(\xvec) = T(T(\xvec))\text{,}\) which is the result of rotating \(\xvec\) by \(90^\circ\) twice.
      1. What is the matrix associated to the composition \((T\circ T)\text{?}\)
        🔗
        🔗
      2. What is the result of rotating \(\vvec=\twovec{-2}{-1}\) twice?
        🔗
        🔗
      3. Suppose that \(R:\real^2\to\real^2\) is the matrix transformation that rotates vectors by \(180^\circ\text{,}\) as shown in Figure 10.
        🔗
        Use the vectors \(\evec_i\) to find the matrix associated to \(R\) and explain why it is the same matrix associated to \((T\circ T)\text{.}\)
        🔗
        🔗
      4. Write the two-dimensional vector \((T\circ T)\left(\twovec xy\right)\text{.}\) How might this vector be expressed in terms of scalar multiplication and why does this make sense geometrically?
        🔗
        🔗

    2. In the previous activity, we imagined a company that operates two bakeries. We found the matrix transformation \(T:\real^2\to\real^3\) where \(T\left(\twovec{x_1}{x_2}\right)\) describes the number of cakes, doughnuts, and eclairs when Bakery1 runs for \(x_1\) hours and Bakery 2 runs for \(x_2\) hours. The associated matrix is \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 10 \amp 20 \\ 50 \amp 30 \\ 30 \amp 30 \\ \end{bmatrix} \text{.}\)
      🔗
      Suppose now that
      • Each cake requires 4 cups of flour and and 2 cups of sugar.
        🔗
        🔗
      • Each doughnut requires 1 cup of flour and 1 cup of sugar.
        🔗
        🔗
      • Each eclair requires 1 cup of flour and 2 cups of sugar.
        🔗
        🔗
      We will describe a matrix transformation \(S:\real^3\to\real^2\) where \(S\left(\threevec{y_1}{y_2}{y_3}\right)\) is a two-dimensional vector describing the number of cups of flour and sugar required to make \(y_1\) cakes, \(y_2\) doughnuts, and \(y_3\) eclairs.
      1. Use the vectors \(\evec_i\) to write the matrix \(B\) associated to the transformation \(S\text{.}\)
        🔗
        🔗
      2. If we make 1200 cakes, 2850 doughnuts, and 2250 eclairs, how many cups of flour and sugar are required?
        🔗
        🔗
      3. Suppose that Bakery 1 operates for 75 hours and Bakery 2 operates for 53 hours. How many cakes, doughnuts, and eclairs are produced? How many cups of flour and sugar are required?
        🔗
        🔗
      4. What is the meaning of the composition \((S\circ T)\) and what is its associated matrix?
        🔗
        🔗
      5. In a certain time interval, both bakeries use a total of 5800 cups of flour and 5980 cups of sugar. How long have the two bakeries been operating?
        🔗
        🔗
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
      1. The matrix is \(B=AA=A^2 = \begin{bmatrix} -1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp -1 \\ \end{bmatrix} \text{.}\)
        🔗
        🔗
      2. \((T\circ T)(\vvec) = A^2\vvec = \twovec21\text{.}\)
        🔗
        🔗
      3. The matrix associated to \(R\) is also \(A^2\) since rotating by \(90^\circ\) twice is the same as rotating once by \(180^\circ\text{.}\)
        🔗
        🔗
      4. \((T\circ T)(\xvec) = R(\xvec) = -\xvec\text{,}\) which makes sense because multiplying a vector by \(-1\) simply changes its direction.
        🔗
        🔗
      🔗
      🔗
      1. \(B = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \amp 1 \amp 1 \\ 2 \amp 1 \amp 2 \\ \end{bmatrix} \text{.}\)
        🔗
        🔗
      2. \(S\left(\threevec{1200}{2850}{2250}\right) = B\threevec{1200}{2850}{2250} = \twovec{9900}{9750}\text{.}\)
        🔗
        🔗
      3. \(T\left(\twovec{75}{53}\right) = \threevec{1810}{5340}{3840}\) and \(S\left(\threevec{1810}{5340}{3840}\right) = \twovec{16430}{16640}\text{.}\)
        🔗
        🔗
      4. \((S\circ T)\) takes as input a vector \(\xvec\) that records the number of hours both bakeries operate and outputs a vector \((S\circ T)(\xvec)\) that tells us the total number of cups of flour and sugar used. The associated matrix is \(BA=\begin{bmatrix} 120 \amp 140 \\ 130 \amp 130 \\ \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\)
        🔗
        🔗
      5. We want to find the vector \(\xvec\) for which \((S\circ T)(\xvec) = BA\xvec = \twovec{5800}{5980}\text{.}\) Solving this equation gives \(\xvec=\twovec{32}{14}\text{.}\)
        🔗
